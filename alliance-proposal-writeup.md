# Local d-AI Summer
### Local Decentralized AI Summer
*A Proposal by the Infinite Front Porch — Presented to the AI Alliance*

Local d-AI Summer is a facilitation program for Canadian social impact organizations navigating the AI landscape. Through peer-driven learning events, hands-on skill-building, and knowledge commoning, participants build genuine fluency in decentralized, community-owned AI tools — and connect with the broader ecosystem of organizations already using them. The program is proposed by the Infinite Front Porch and delivered in partnership with the AI Alliance and aligned web3 contributor networks.

---

## What We're Offering

The Infinite Front Porch proposes to facilitate learning and knowledge commoning across the AI Alliance and the broader social impact ecosystem. The program has two complementary tracks: **Local d-AI Summer** (learning events and peer support) and the **Infinite Front Porch** (technical senseweaving and knowledge graphing).

---

### Local d-AI Summer — Learning Events & Peer Support

A guided journey through the decentralized AI landscape, focused on community ownership and data sovereignty from a Canadian bioregional context. Sessions are held on Zoom or comparable open platforms; recordings and notes are shared in a commons accessible to all participants.

Alongside scheduled sessions, the program offers **weekly drop-in coworking hours** hosted by Public Goods network contributors and Alliance members — open space for questions, troubleshooting, and peer problem-solving. Participants can also book one-on-one calls for focused support, and gain access to contributor networks to help evaluate and deploy knowledge resources and open-source AI tooling.

#### Introductory Sessions

Accessible sessions introducing key concepts and making space for Alliance members and community representatives to share their own experiences and knowledge:

- **What Is AI, Actually?** — What are algorithms? What is data, and how do you own it? How do language models work, what can they do reliably, and how do you evaluate a tool for community use?
- **Community Perspectives on AI** — Facilitated space for Alliance members and community representatives to share what they're seeing, what they're trying, and what they're worried about. The goal is to common this knowledge across our network, not to deliver formal case studies.
- **What's Already Happening Here** — A shared look at the social AI work already underway across the Alliance and Canada's bioregions, surfaced through member contributions rather than formal research or policy review.

#### Skills Sessions

Practical, jargon-free instruction for participants with no prior technical experience. Each session is designed so someone can leave with working knowledge and a concrete next step to try on their own:

- **Filesystems and Markdown** — How digital knowledge is stored, structured, and made portable; plain-text formats that let you own your content regardless of what platform you use
- **Terminals, Editors, and AI Coding Assistants** — How to navigate a command line, use a basic code editor, and actually work with an AI coding tool in practice — focused on what you can do with these today, not how they work under the hood
- **Owning Your Data and Infrastructure** — Practical frameworks for understanding who controls what in any technology stack, and how to choose tools that keep your organization in the driver's seat

#### Technical Deep-Dives

More substantive sessions co-designed with Alliance members based on expressed needs, targeting specific challenges and opportunities in the social AI space:

- **Sharing Sessions with Alliance Builders** — Facilitated peer exchange to surface existing work, identify common problems, and reduce redundant effort across the network
- **Workshops with Subject Matter Experts** — Structured sessions with practitioners from the decentralized AI ecosystem on specific approaches and common pain points: local model deployment, federated data, open-source agent frameworks, and more
- **Knowledge Mobilization Labs** — Helping participating organizations work with their own information stewards to move community knowledge into shared, interoperable formats that can be queried, linked, and built upon

### Infinite Front Porch — Technical Senseweaving & Knowledge Graphing

The most persistent barrier social impact organizations face with new technology isn't a lack of intelligence — it's a translation problem. Leadership understands the organization's needs; they just can't always articulate them in language that produces useful search results, meaningful vendor conversations, or coherent briefs for technical contributors. And technical contributors often miss the organizational, relational, and political dimensions of what they're being asked to build.

**Technical Senseweaving** is a facilitated service for closing that gap.

#### Problem Translation

Working with organizational leaders and frontline staff together, our contributors translate abstractly-stated operational challenges into specific, searchable technical language. This gives communities the vocabulary to find existing solutions, evaluate what they're being offered, and communicate across contexts. We also validate top-down statements of need against worker and community experience — because misaligned understanding between leadership and the people doing the work is the single most common reason technology projects fail before they start.

#### Knowledge Graphing & Gardening

The Infinite Front Porch maintains and extends a living knowledge graph of the social tech ecosystem, rooted in Canada's bioregions and connected to global knowledge commons. For Alliance members, this includes:

- **Bioregional social tech mapping** — A navigable graph of who is doing what, where, and with what technology across Canada's bioregions, so Alliance members can find peers, surface collaboration opportunities, and avoid duplicating work that's already been done
- **Alliance ecosystem graphing on Geo Protocol** — Asset mapping and knowledge graphing of the AI Alliance's own ecosystem, making its collective knowledge legible and connectable to other commons at regional and global scale
- **Knowledge commons connections** — Direct introductions to bioregional and domain-specific knowledge networks, so organizations can plug into existing stewardship infrastructure rather than building from scratch

## Resourcing

We propose a shared cost model: hands-on facilitation funded by participants, programming funded by sponsors, and labor contributed by the network.

### Labor

The program is delivered by a distributed contributor network coordinated through an ephemeral DAO structure, drawing from:

- Individual contributors from AI Alliance member organizations
- Individual contributors from aligned web3 labor swarms:
  - **Green Pill Network** — decentralized public goods funding and regenerative crypto economics
  - **Bread Cooperative** — cooperative technology and economic democracy
  - **OpenCivics Labs** — open civic innovation frameworks and social tech stack knowledge
  - **Gaia AI** — AI agent software and deployment frameworks for bioregional and local community contexts

### Funding

The program targets a combined budget of **CAD $55,000–$115,000**, with costs shared across participant co-funding (CAD $30,000–$50,000) and web3 sponsorship (USD $25,000–$50,000).

#### Participant Co-Funding

Pay-what-you-can model for AI Alliance members and participating organizations, with a target of **CAD $30,000–$50,000** (reflecting 3–5 contributors at CAD $10,000 each). In-kind contributions are recognized and valued, but do not count toward the cash goal.

Co-funders receive, or may gift to a team member:

- **Prioritized input on learning program topic selection** — co-funders shape the curriculum around their communities' most pressing needs
- **A dedicated, managed IronClaw instance** — a privacy-preserving AI assistant configured for the organization's own knowledge management tasks
- **Additional one-on-one and group support** beyond the standard program offerings

#### Web3 Sponsorship

To fund programming, infrastructure, and contributor compensation, we will pursue sponsorship from aligned web3 projects with a combined target of **USD $25,000–$50,000** — prospective sponsors include Near Protocol, Anoma, Celo, and comparable networks.

Sponsors receive:

- Input on learning content related to their domain, with opportunities to speak directly to Alliance members via live events
- Prominent brand placement in relevant learning session content
- Promotion of their protocol or infrastructure to organizations with reputable social impact mandates and real potential for large-scale deployments
- **Free Bioregional Builder slots** for AI infrastructure and knowledge projects based in Canada's bioregions — for equity, goodwill, and network development

#### Governance of Project Resources

Contributors co-steward project resources through an ephemeral DAO — a purpose-built, time-limited governance structure dissolved when the project concludes. All financial flows are managed on distributed public ledgers, providing transparent, auditable accounting to co-funders and participants. Communication and reporting protocols will be established by mutual agreement and documented in the shared knowledge commons.

## Our Request

We are asking the AI Alliance for three things:

**1. Commitments to co-funding**
Even soft, conditional commitments allow us to approach web3 sponsors with a credible signal of community demand. We're asking for expressions of intent and an honest conversation about what funding levels are realistic for your organization.

**2. Permission to name the AI Alliance in sponsor outreach**
The Alliance's credibility is a genuine asset in these conversations. With your permission, we would identify the Alliance as a program partner in web3 outreach, and bring any formal partnership agreements back for review before signing.

**3. Feedback and topic requests**
What are the AI questions your members are losing sleep over? What skills gaps are most acute? What domains — food sovereignty, housing, climate adaptation, cooperative finance — are most represented in your network? Your input shapes the curriculum, and early input shapes it best.
